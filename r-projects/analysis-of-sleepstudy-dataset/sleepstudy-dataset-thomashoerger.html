1 Introduction

This report presents an analysis of sleep patterns among college students, utilizing the SleepStudy dataset obtained from https://www.lock5stat.com/datapage3e.html. The dataset comprises 253 observations on 27 variables, providing valuable insights into the sleep habits, psychological well-being, and lifestyle choices of college students.

The primary objective of this analysis is to examine the relationships between sleep behaviors and academic performance, as well as psychological and lifestyle variables among college students. By addressing these relationships, we aim to uncover patterns that could inform interventions to improve student well-being and academic success.

The following research questions will be addressed:

  1. Is there a significant difference in the average GPA between male and female college students?
  2. Is there a significant difference in the average number of early classes between the first two class years and other class years?
  3. Do students who identify as “larks” have significantly better cognitive skills (CognitionZscore) compared to “owls”?
  4. Is there a significant difference in the average number of classes missed between students who had at least one early class and those who didn’t?
  5. Is there a significant difference in the average happiness level between students with at least moderate depression and those with normal depression status?
  6. Is there a significant difference in sleep quality scores between students who reported having at least one all-nighter and those who didn’t?
  7. Do students who abstain from alcohol use have significantly better stress scores than those who report heavy alcohol use?
  8. Is there a significant difference in the average number of drinks per week between students of different genders?
  9. Is there a significant difference in the average weekday bedtime between students with high and low stress?
  10. Is there a significant difference in the average hours of sleep on weekends between first two year students and other students?

By exploring these questions, this report aims to contribute valuable insights into the factors that influence college student sleep patterns and their broader impact.

2 Data

The SleepStudy dataset contains 253 observations and 27 variables. These include:

  • Demographics: Gender, ClassYear
  • Academic performance: GPA, ClassesMissed
  • Sleep behavior: WeekdayBed, WeekendSleep, NumEarlyClass, AllNighter
  • Psychological well-being: DepressionScore, AnxietyScore, StressScore, DepressionStatus
  • Lifestyle variables: AlcoholUse, Drinks per week, LarkOwl chronotype

3 Analysis

3.1 RQ1: GPA by Gender

This research question investigates whether there is a significant difference in the average GPA between male and female college students. Gender was coded as a categorical variable, and a two-sample t-test was used to compare the mean GPA across gender groups.

# Make sure Gender is a factor
sleep_data$Gender <- as.factor(sleep_data$Gender)

# Boxplot
ggplot(sleep_data, aes(x = Gender, y = GPA, fill = Gender)) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  labs(title = "GPA by Gender", x = "Gender", y = "GPA")

Conclusion: A two-sample t-test revealed a statistically significant difference in GPA between male and female students (t = 3.91, df = 200.9, p = 0.0001).

Mean GPA (Male): 3.32

Mean GPA (Female): 3.12

95% Confidence Interval for the difference: [0.10, 0.30]

This result suggests that male students had a significantly higher average GPA than female students in this sample.

3.2 RQ2: Number of Early Classes by Class Year Group

This research question investigates whether underclassmen (FirstYear and Sophomore students) have a significantly different number of early classes compared to upperclassmen (Junior and Senior students). Students were grouped into two categories: EarlyYears (FirstYear and Sophomore) and Other (Junior and Senior). A two-sample t-test was used to compare the average number of early classes between these two groups.

# Re-import the original dataset (optional but safe)
sleep_data <- read.csv("SleepStudy.csv")

# Recode ClassYear from 1-4 to labels
sleep_data$ClassYear <- factor(sleep_data$ClassYear,
                               levels = c(1, 2, 3, 4),
                               labels = c("FirstYear", "Sophomore", "Junior", "Senior"))

# Create ClassGroup
sleep_data$ClassGroup <- ifelse(sleep_data$ClassYear %in% c("FirstYear", "Sophomore"),
                                "EarlyYears", "Other")

# Filter only rows with valid NumEarlyClass and ClassGroup
rq2_data <- sleep_data %>%
  filter(!is.na(NumEarlyClass),
         ClassGroup %in% c("EarlyYears", "Other")) %>%
  mutate(ClassGroup = factor(ClassGroup)) %>%
  droplevels()


# Boxplot
ggplot(rq2_data, aes(x = ClassGroup, y = NumEarlyClass, fill = ClassGroup)) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  labs(title = "Number of Early Classes by Class Year Group",
       x = "Class Group", y = "Number of Early Classes")

Conclusion: A Welch two-sample t-test showed a statistically significant difference in the number of early classes between underclassmen and upperclassmen (t = 4.18, df = 250.7, p < 0.001).

Mean (EarlyYears): 2.07 early classes

Mean (Other): 1.31 early classes

95% Confidence Interval for the difference: [0.40, 1.12]

This suggests that students in their first two years of college tend to take significantly more early classes than upperclassmen.

3.3 RQ3: CognitionZscore by Lark/Owl Type

This research question explores whether students who identify as “larks” have significantly better cognitive skills, measured by their Cognition Z-Score, compared to those who identify as “owls.” Students who selected “Neither” were excluded, and a two-sample t-test was used to compare the average cognition scores between the two chronotype groups.

# Filter out "Neither" and keep only Lark and Owl

  geom_boxplot() +
  labs(title = "Cognition Z-Score by Chronotype (Lark vs Owl)",
       x = "Chronotype", y = "Cognition Z-Score")

Conclusion: A Welch two-sample t-test found no statistically significant difference in cognition z-scores between larks and owls (t = 0.81, df = 75.33, p = 0.423).

Mean (Lark): 0.09

Mean (Owl): -0.04

95% Confidence Interval for the difference: [-0.19, 0.45]

These results suggest that chronotype (being a lark or an owl) does not significantly impact cognitive performance in this sample.

4 Summary

This report explored ten research questions focused on the sleep patterns, lifestyle choices, and academic performance of college students using the SleepStudy dataset.

Key findings from the analysis include:

  • RQ1: Male students had significantly higher GPAs than female students.
  • RQ2: Underclassmen (FirstYear and Sophomore) took significantly more early classes than upperclassmen (Junior and Senior).
  • RQ3: There was no significant difference in cognition scores between students who identified as “larks” and those who identified as “owls.”
  • RQ4: There was no significant difference in the number of classes missed between students with and without early classes.
  • RQ5: Students with moderate or severe depression reported significantly lower happiness scores than students with normal depression status.
  • RQ6: Although students who pulled all-nighters reported slightly worse sleep quality, the difference was not statistically significant.
  • RQ7: There was no significant difference in stress scores between students who abstain from alcohol and those who report heavy alcohol use.
  • RQ8: Female students reported significantly more alcoholic drinks per week than male students.
  • RQ9: Stress level was not significantly associated with weekday bedtime.
  • RQ10: There was no significant difference in weekend sleep duration between underclassmen and upperclassmen.

4.1 Implications

These findings suggest that while some lifestyle factors—such as class year and depression status—are significantly associated with academic or mental health outcomes, others (like alcohol use and sleep patterns) showed weaker or non-significant associations. Notably, the strong link between depression and happiness, and between early class scheduling and class year, highlight areas that may warrant further exploration or support interventions for students.

4.2 Overall Conclusion

The SleepStudy dataset provides valuable insight into the behaviors and well-being of college students. Although not all relationships were statistically significant, this analysis helps identify which factors are most strongly associated with academic performance and mental health, contributing to a better understanding of student success and well-being.
